Starting a YouTube channel or streaming career is a goal for many digital creators worldwide. However, the barrier to entry is often hardware. Playing a modern game is demanding enough on an older laptop or a budget PC with 4GB or 8GB of RAM. The moment you hit the "Record" button, the system can struggle to keep up. Your smooth gameplay may drop to a low 15 FPS, audio can desync, and your computer overheats noticeably.

If you have experienced this, you are not alone. The internet is full of tutorials promising quick fixes, but they rarely address the root cause of the performance issue. At BSR Studios, we focus on optimizing the software itself.

In this comprehensive guide, we will explore the mechanics behind video encoding, explain why standard screen recorders impact performance, and show you how to record efficiently on budget hardware.

1. The Struggle of Content Creation on Budget PCs

Content creation demands significant processing power. A high-end setup usually consists of a powerful multi-core CPU and a dedicated graphics card. This allows the system to split tasks efficiently: the GPU renders the 3D graphics of the game, while a dedicated chip (like NVENC) handles video compression in real-time.

But what if you are using an Intel i3 processor with integrated graphics? Your system is likely using most of its capacity just to run the game. Asking it to simultaneously encode high-definition video at 60 frames per second is a heavy burden. This is where severe stuttering originates.

2. Why Traditional Recorders Reduce Your FPS

OBS (Open Broadcaster Software) Studio is the industry standard for screen recording and streaming. It is an excellent piece of software. However, it is primarily built for modern hardware. It uses a method called "Real-Time Video Compression".

When you record with standard encoders, the software analyzes every frame, compresses it, and writes it to an MP4 file on your drive. This requires a significant amount of CPU cycles. If your CPU is already maxed out by running a game, the recording software has to compete for resources, leading to dropped frames.

"You cannot force a budget CPU to handle real-time video encoding without impacting game performance. An effective solution is to separate the encoding process from the gameplay phase."

3. The Myth of "Game Boosters"

Out of desperation, many users download "Game Boosters." These programs claim to free up RAM and CPU usage. Ironically, these boosters are often heavy software that run continuously in the background, consuming the very resources they are meant to save.

Moreover, a booster cannot physically give your CPU the ability to encode video faster. True optimization comes from using fundamentally efficient software.

5. How "Smart Delta Frame" Technology Works

Any Screen Recorder uses native Windows APIs to hook directly into your graphics output. When you record, it does not immediately encode an MP4. Instead, it captures raw data and saves it instantly to a temporary .screc file.

It also features a "Smart Delta Frame" logic. If you are reading a static menu in a game, traditional recorders still record 60 identical frames per second. Our software detects this idle state and pauses capture, keeping CPU usage minimal. It only captures the pixels that change.

Because there is no heavy compression happening during gameplay, your CPU is free to focus on the game itself. Once you finish playing, you can use the software to convert that raw .screc file into a smooth MP4 video at your convenience.

6. Step-by-Step Guide to Smooth Recording

Follow these steps to learn how to achieve consistent 60FPS recordings on your PC:

  1. Optimize Windows: Turn off "Xbox Game Bar" and background recording in Windows settings, as this native feature constantly records the screen and impacts performance.
  2. Download the Tool: Install Any Screen Recorder from our official Apps page.
  3. Configure In-Game Settings: Lower your in-game shadows and anti-aliasing. Capping the frame rate in the game settings to a stable 30 FPS or 60 FPS provides better recording consistency.
  4. Start Recording: Open Any Screen Recorder, select your resolution and frame rate, and click Start. The app will capture raw files in the background.
  5. The Conversion: After gaming, open the app, select your recent recording, and click "Turn into Video" to render the final MP4 file.

Pro Tip: Use an SSD

Because Any Screen Recorder writes raw data during gameplay, it requires adequate disk write speeds. Saving your recordings to an SSD (Solid State Drive) will help ensure the smoothest experience possible.

7. Capturing Clear Internal Audio

A great gaming video needs great audio. Any Screen Recorder utilizes the native Windows WASAPI loopback engine to intercept sound exactly as it leaves your game, resulting in clear, high-quality internal audio capture. It syncs with your raw video data during the final MP4 conversion process. It also features a dedicated "Audio Only" mode.
